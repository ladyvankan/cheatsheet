Things to Remember

HTML Structure

Tag Description Additional Info
<!DOCTYPE html> It tells the browser that your document is written in HTML5. This is the very first line of your document.
<html></html> This is the container for all other HTML elements. This is the second/last line of your document.?
<head></head> Where all of your document's important info goes, including stylesheets and the title. Get witty with your title; this is what users will see in their browser's tab.
<body></body> The almighty holder of what will actually go on your webpage. *hint,hint* If you color your body with background-color, it will affect your entire webpage's background.
<main></main> For the *main* content of your page. Like, you know, what your page is actually about.
<header></header> A comfy place to store the title of your page on your actual page. Don't forget about your <nav> bar! There's more than enough room for it here too.
<footer></footer> Another comfy place, but for anything you want to hide at the bottom of your page. Perfect for copyright, FAQs, or pretty little signatures.
<section></section> Use this one when you want to group like-minded information together. Contrary to popular belief, this can also go inside an <article>.
<article></article> This is for those paragraphs that carry enough info on their own to be understood without the rest of your page. Think "newspapers" and you'll be golden.

HTML Tags

Tag Description Additional Info
<img src="" alt=""> Welcome to our very first *self-closing* element! This is the perfect tag for all of your beautiful pictures. src="" is where your image lives, while alt="" is two-fold — this is what shows if your picture doesn't load, and it's also what screen readers read.
<h1></h1> The best way to display the title on your actual page. "There can be only one."

<p></p> This is where all of your paragraphs go! Use <br> (our second self-closing tag) if you want to start sentences on a new line.
<span></span> This is for those special words that just HAVE to be a different style or color. *hint,hint* This is how I made all of those pretty tags pink in the middle of my sentences.
<div></div> The very UN-semantic way of splitting up your page to design sections differently. Try *not* to use this one unless you absolutely need to.
<aside></aside> The a*side* information that doesn't really add "real" information to your page. The perfect tag for quotes or things that don't aboslutely need to be present.

CSS Style

Property Name Description Additional Info
color For coloring your text. Beautify with color names, rgb values, and hsl values.
background-color For the, you know, background. Same as color.
font-size Need I say more? Can be specified with both relative and absolute units.
font-family Font style, just like a word processor. There are many different styles to choose from on the line. Enter at your own risk.
border Combine border-width, border-style, and border-color in one impressive package! Choose from a semi-wide variety of border styles such as solid, dotted, and dashed!
border-radius Rounds das corners. Want a circle? Mix equal parts height and width with the same size radius. Ta-da!
font-weight A fancy way to thicken your words, by weight. Not to be confused with the inline element of <strong>, which also boldens your words.
